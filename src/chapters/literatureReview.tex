\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW}
Smart building sensors are moving away from simple motion detection toward high-fidelity computer vision. This shift is necessary because traditional hardware, like Passive Infrared (PIR) and Radar, often fails to detect stationary people. In fact, research by Ahmad et al. \cite{Ahmad2021} shows that PIR sensors have a 0.0\% reliability when an occupant sits still, as they rely entirely on thermal fluctuations. While PC-based models can reach an 85.0\% Mean Average Precision (mAP), they are too bulky for cheap hardware. To bridge this gap, the project uses INT8 quantization. This strategy, as detailed by Nagel et al. \cite{nagel2021white}, shrinks the model’s memory footprint by four times while keeping 99.7\% accuracy. By following the ESP32-S3 hardware specifications \cite{esp32s3_datasheet}, we can fit these algorithms into the device's tiny 512 KB SRAM limit. Furthermore, Wu et al. \cite{wu2016qcnn} proved in their Q-CNN research that focusing on matching the output of quantized layers to their floating-point originals allows for 20x compression. This is a vital step to fix the slow 800 ms to 1200 ms latency often found in standard edge devices.

Efficiency also comes from the way the neural network architecture is designed. Iandola et al. \cite{iandola2016squeezenet} introduced SqueezeNet, which uses Fire Modules to replace expensive $3 \times 3$ filters with $1 \times 1$ squeeze layers, cutting parameters drastically without losing AlexNet-level precision. Taking this further, Juefei-Xu et al. \cite{juefei2017lbcnn} developed Local Binary Convolutional Neural Networks (LBCNN). By replacing standard learnable filters with fixed  sparse binary kernels, they achieved a 169x parameter reduction making deep learning feasible on low-power chips. 

This project also tackles the Information Loss Gap. Instead of shrinking a 4K image and making it blurry, we use data partitioning via tiling. Li et al. \cite{li2020pytorch} demonstrated that splitting images into a grid preserves the high-frequency spatial details needed for accurate human detection. Although the SAHI framework by Akyon et al. \cite{akyon2022sahi} can boost small-object detection accuracy by 20 to 30\% through slicing, it usually requires a powerful, offline workstation. This project fills that gap by using the ESP-NOW protocol to distribute these tiles across a cluster. As benchmarked by Jeong et al. \cite{jeong2021}, this protocol keeps peer-to-peer communication latency as low as 1.115 ms. By combining LBCNN structural efficiency with this distributed tiling architecture, we create a low-cost, private alternative to expensive industrial monitoring systems.
